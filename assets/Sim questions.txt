Mitral Stenosis

Mitral Regurgitation – PV Loop Questions

HFrEF

1.

A patient with chronic HFrEF has a decreased ejection fraction. Which of the following physiological changes is likely to be seen?
A. Increased end-systolic volume
B. Increased contractility
C. Decreased preload
D. Increased stroke volume

Correct answer: A. Increased end-systolic volume (ESV)
In HFrEF, systolic dysfunction → the ventricle cannot fully eject blood → ESV increases, lowering EF.

Why the others are wrong
B Increased contractility → Does heart failure with reduced ejection fraction present with increased or decreased contractility?
C Decreased preload → HFrEF usually has increased preload due to fluid retention. Think about what would happen with leftover blood in a heart with reduced contractility.
D Increased stroke volume → Think again - stroke volume decreases in HFrEF due to reduced contractility.

2.

In HFrEF, which compensatory mechanism initially helps maintain cardiac output?
A. Decreased sympathetic tone
Reconsider. In heart failure, baroreceptor unloading leads to increased, not decreased, sympathetic activation to support heart rate and contractility.
B. Increased parasympathetic tone
Reconsider. Vagal tone is withdrawn in HFrEF to allow tachycardia and higher cardiac output.
C. Increased renin release
Correct. Reduced renal perfusion activates the RAAS. Increased renin ultimately raises blood volume, increasing preload and temporarily supporting cardiac output via the Frank–Starling mechanism.
D. Decreased venous return
Reconsider. Heart failure is associated with fluid retention, which increases venous return rather than decreasing it.

3.

A patient with HFrEF shows a leftward/downward shift of the end-systolic pressure–volume relationship (ESPVR). What does this indicate?
A. Increased contractility
Reconsider. Increased contractility makes the ESPVR steeper and shifts it upward, not downward.
B. Decreased contractility
Correct. A reduced ESPVR slope reflects impaired inotropy, which is a defining feature of HFrEF.
C. Increased afterload
Reconsider. Afterload alters pressure requirements but does not change the slope of the ESPVR, which reflects intrinsic contractility.
D. Increased stroke volume
Reconsider. Stroke volume is typically reduced in HFrEF due to increased ESV.

4.

What is the primary mechanism responsible for pulmonary edema in left-sided HFrEF?
A. Increased right ventricular contractility
Reconsider. Enhanced RV function would tend to relieve, not worsen, pulmonary congestion.
B. Increased left atrial pressure due to elevated LV end-diastolic pressure
Correct. LV failure causes blood to back up into the left atrium and pulmonary veins, raising pulmonary capillary hydrostatic pressure and leading to edema.
C. Decreased blood volume
Reconsider. Most patients with HFrEF are volume overloaded, not hypovolemic.
D. High oncotic pressure
Reconsider. Increased oncotic pressure would oppose fluid movement into the lungs.

5.

Why is afterload commonly increased in patients with HFrEF?
A. Increased nitric oxide production
Reconsider. Nitric oxide causes vasodilation, which lowers afterload.
B. RAAS activation leading to vasoconstriction
Correct. Angiotensin II increases systemic vascular resistance, raising afterload and worsening ventricular emptying.
C. Low sympathetic tone
Reconsider. Sympathetic tone is elevated, not low, in HFrEF.
D. Decreased α₁-adrenergic receptor activation
Reconsider. α₁ stimulation is increased, contributing to vasoconstriction.

6.

Which pressure–volume loop change is characteristic of HFrEF?
A. Increased ESPVR slope
Reconsider. ESPVR slope decreases in systolic dysfunction due to impaired contractility.
B. Rightward shift of EDV (increased preload)
Correct. Neurohormonal activation leads to sodium and water retention, increasing preload and shifting EDV to the right.
C. Higher peak systolic pressures
Reconsider. Peak systolic pressure is often reduced in HFrEF.
D. Increased ESV with decreased EDV
Reconsider. Both ESV and EDV increase in HFrEF.

7.

A patient with HFrEF has reduced forward stroke volume. Which renal mechanism contributes to disease progression?
A. Decreased aldosterone
Reconsider. Aldosterone secretion is increased, not decreased.
B. Increased natriuresis
Reconsider. Sodium retention predominates and worsens congestion.
C. RAAS activation causing sodium and water retention
Correct. Although initially compensatory, chronic fluid retention increases preload and venous congestion, worsening symptoms.
D. Increased renal blood flow
Reconsider. Renal perfusion is typically reduced in HFrEF.

8.

Why does ejection fraction remain reduced in HFrEF despite increased preload?
A. Flattening of the Frank–Starling curve
Correct. With severely impaired contractility, additional preload produces minimal increases in stroke volume.
B. Inadequate venous return
Reconsider. Venous return is often elevated.
C. Chronically low afterload
Reconsider. Afterload is frequently increased.
D. Increased ventricular compliance
Reconsider. Compliance may be normal or increased, but EF reduction is driven by impaired inotropy.

9.

Chronic sympathetic activation in HFrEF leads to which maladaptive effect?
A. Decreased myocardial oxygen demand
Reconsider. Tachycardia and increased wall stress increase oxygen demand.
B. Downregulation of β₁-adrenergic receptors
Correct. Persistent catecholamine exposure leads to receptor downregulation and worsened systolic function.
C. Sustained increase in cardiac output
Reconsider. Any increase in CO is temporary and declines over time.
D. Decreased renin release
Reconsider. Sympathetic stimulation increases renin secretion.

10.

Why do ACE inhibitors improve survival in patients with HFrEF?
A. They increase afterload
Reconsider. ACE inhibitors reduce systemic vascular resistance.
B. They reduce both preload and afterload
Correct. Reduced angiotensin II lowers vasoconstriction and aldosterone-mediated fluid retention, improving hemodynamics and survival.
C. They decrease serum potassium
Reconsider. ACE inhibitors can cause hyperkalemia.
D. They increase sympathetic tone
Reconsider. ACE inhibitors blunt maladaptive neurohormonal activation.

HFpEF

1.

A patient with long-standing hypertension develops dyspnea. Echo shows normal EF but thickened LV walls. What mechanism best explains her symptoms?
A. Increased LV compliance
B. Decreased LV end-diastolic pressure
C. Impaired ventricular relaxation
D. Increased ventricular filling time

Correct answer: C — Impaired ventricular relaxation
HFpEF = diastolic dysfunction due to a stiff, hypertrophied LV that cannot relax properly → impaired filling → ↑ filling pressures.

Click-feedback (AMBOSS-style):
A. Increased LV compliance
Reorient your thinking: Hypertrophy makes the ventricle stiffer, not more compliant.

B. Decreased LV end-diastolic pressure
Reorient your thinking: If filling is impaired, the ventricle requires higher, not lower, pressure to fill.

D. Increased ventricular filling time
Reorient your thinking: Time is not the limiting factor — stiffness and relaxation are.

2.

Which change is most characteristic on the PV loop of HFpEF?
A. Rightward shift of ESPVR
B. Upward/leftward shift of EDPVR
C. Increased stroke volume
D. Decreased end-diastolic pressure

Correct answer: B — Upward/leftward shift of EDPVR
A stiff ventricle reaches higher pressures at lower volumes during diastole.

Click-feedback:
A. Rightward shift of ESPVR
Reorient your thinking: ESPVR reflects contractility, which is preserved in HFpEF.

C. Increased stroke volume
Reorient your thinking: Poor filling → lower EDV → lower SV.

D. Decreased end-diastolic pressure
Reorient your thinking: HFpEF is defined by elevated filling pressures.

3.

HFpEF most commonly results from:
A. Chronic aortic regurgitation
B. Hypertension causing concentric LV hypertrophy
C. Dilated cardiomyopathy
D. Viral myocarditis

Correct answer: B — LV hypertrophy from HTN
Chronic pressure overload → concentric hypertrophy → stiff ventricle → diastolic failure.

Click-feedback:
A. Chronic aortic regurgitation
Reorient your thinking: AR causes volume overload and eccentric hypertrophy, leading to HFrEF.

C. Dilated cardiomyopathy
Reorient your thinking: Dilated ventricles impair systolic function (low EF).

D. Viral myocarditis
Reorient your thinking: Myocardial damage primarily affects contractility, not relaxation.

4.

Which measurement confirms preserved systolic function?
A. Decreased EF
B. Normal EF
C. Reduced stroke volume
D. Increased end-systolic volume (ESV)

Correct answer: B — Normal EF
HFpEF = heart failure with preserved ejection fraction.

Click-feedback:
A. Decreased EF
Reorient your thinking: A low EF defines HFrEF, not HFpEF.

C. Reduced stroke volume
Reorient your thinking: SV may fall due to low EDV, but EF can remain normal.

D. Increased ESV
Reorient your thinking: ESV rises with impaired systolic emptying, which is not the problem here.

5.

Which hemodynamic finding is characteristic of HFpEF?
A. Low LV filling pressures
B. High LV end-diastolic pressure
C. Increased LV chamber volume
D. Decreased LV wall thickness

Correct answer: B — High LVEDP
A stiff ventricle requires high pressure to fill, backing up into the lungs.

Click-feedback:
A. Low LV filling pressures
Reorient your thinking: Pulmonary congestion requires elevated filling pressures.

C. Increased LV chamber volume
Reorient your thinking: HFpEF typically has normal or reduced chamber size.

D. Decreased LV wall thickness
Reorient your thinking: HFpEF is associated with concentric hypertrophy.

6.

On the PV loop, HFpEF produces which change?
A. Wide loop with increased SV
B. Narrow loop with decreased EDV
C. Downward shift of EDPVR
D. Decreased afterload

Correct answer: B — Narrow loop with decreased EDV
Impaired filling → ↓ EDV → ↓ SV → narrow loop.

Click-feedback:
A. Wide loop with increased SV
Reorient your thinking: Wide loops imply good filling, which HFpEF lacks.

C. Downward shift of EDPVR
Reorient your thinking: Decreased compliance shifts the curve upward, not downward.

D. Decreased afterload
Reorient your thinking: Afterload is not the primary abnormality in HFpEF.

7.

In HFpEF, what happens during early diastole?
A. Rapid early filling increases
B. Passive filling decreases
C. LV pressure falls faster than normal
D. Pulmonary congestion improves

Correct answer: B — Passive filling decreases
Impaired relaxation → ↓ early filling → ↑ reliance on atrial kick.

Click-feedback:
A. Rapid early filling increases
Reorient your thinking: Relaxation is impaired, so early filling is reduced.

C. LV pressure falls faster than normal
Reorient your thinking: Relaxation is delayed, slowing pressure decline.

D. Pulmonary congestion improves
Reorient your thinking: Elevated filling pressures worsen congestion.

8.

Which physical exam finding is most consistent with HFpEF?
A. Bounding pulses
B. S3 gallop
C. S4 gallop
D. Wide pulse pressure

Correct answer: C — S4
S4 = atrial contraction against a stiff ventricle.
Mnemonic: S3 = di-la-ted (3 syllables), S4 = hy-per-tro-phy (4 syllables).

Click-feedback:
A. Bounding pulses
Reorient your thinking: Bounding pulses suggest high stroke volume states.

B. S3 gallop
Reorient your thinking: S3 is associated with volume overload and dilation.

D. Wide pulse pressure
Reorient your thinking: Seen in aortic regurgitation, not HFpEF.

9.

During exercise, a patient with HFpEF develops pulmonary edema. Why?
A. Increased compliance of the ventricle
B. Dramatic fall in LV filling pressures
C. LV cannot accommodate increased venous return
D. Increased contractility worsens EF

Correct answer: C — Stiff LV cannot handle more preload
Exercise ↑ venous return → LV cannot relax → ↑ LA pressure → pulmonary edema.

Click-feedback:
A. Increased compliance of the ventricle
Reorient your thinking: HFpEF is defined by decreased compliance.

B. Dramatic fall in LV filling pressures
Reorient your thinking: Filling pressures rise, causing edema.

D. Increased contractility worsens EF
Reorient your thinking: Contractility is not the limiting factor; EF is preserved.

10.

Which therapy most directly targets the primary defect in HFpEF?
A. Positive inotropes
B. Afterload reduction / BP control
C. Large fluid boluses
D. Volume expansion to increase preload

Correct answer: B — Afterload reduction & BP control
Treat the cause: hypertension → LV hypertrophy → stiffness.

Click-feedback:
A. Positive inotropes
Reorient your thinking: Contractility is preserved; inotropes add little benefit.

C. Large fluid boluses
Reorient your thinking: Increasing preload worsens pulmonary congestion.

D. Volume expansion to increase preload
Reorient your thinking: The problem is inability to accommodate preload, not lack of it.

Hypertrophic Cardiomyopathy

1.

A 22-year-old athlete collapses during practice. He has a harsh systolic murmur that increases with standing. What physiologic change explains this?
A. Decreased preload → worsens LVOT obstruction
B. Increased preload
C. Increased afterload
D. Increased LV cavity size

Correct answer: A — Decreased preload
Standing ↓ venous return → LV cavity becomes smaller → septum and mitral valve approximate → dynamic LVOT obstruction worsens → murmur increases.

Click-feedback (AMBOSS-style):
B. Increased preload
Reorient your thinking: More preload enlarges the LV cavity and relieves obstruction, softening the murmur.

C. Increased afterload
Reorient your thinking: Higher afterload keeps the LV cavity open longer and reduces obstruction.

D. Increased LV cavity size
Reorient your thinking: Standing makes the LV smaller, not larger.

2.

Which maneuver decreases the intensity of the HOCM murmur?
A. Valsalva
B. Standing
C. Squatting
D. Diuretics

Correct answer: C — Squatting
Squatting ↑ preload and ↑ afterload → LV cavity enlarges → LVOT opens → murmur decreases.

Click-feedback:
A. Valsalva
Reorient your thinking: Valsalva ↓ preload, which worsens obstruction.

B. Standing
Reorient your thinking: Standing ↓ venous return, making the murmur louder.

D. Diuretics
Reorient your thinking: Diuretics reduce preload → smaller LV cavity → worse murmur.

3.

The primary pathophysiologic problem in HOCM is:
A. Systolic dysfunction
B. Dilated LV
C. Dynamic LV outflow tract obstruction due to septal hypertrophy
D. Increased LV compliance

Correct answer: C
Asymmetric septal hypertrophy narrows the LVOT, especially when the LV cavity is small.

Click-feedback:
A. Systolic dysfunction
Reorient your thinking: EF is usually normal or supranormal in HOCM.

B. Dilated LV
Reorient your thinking: HOCM ventricles are small and thick, not dilated.

D. Increased LV compliance
Reorient your thinking: Hypertrophy causes a stiff, noncompliant ventricle.

4.

What causes the systolic anterior motion (SAM) of the mitral valve in HOCM?
A. Increased afterload
B. Venturi effect pulling the mitral leaflet toward the septum
C. Volume overload
D. Fibrosis of the mitral valve

Correct answer: B
Fast blood flow through the narrowed LVOT creates a suction force that pulls the anterior mitral leaflet into the outflow tract.

Click-feedback:
A. Increased afterload
Reorient your thinking: Afterload does not cause SAM; flow dynamics do.

C. Volume overload
Reorient your thinking: HOCM is a low-volume state.

D. Fibrosis of the mitral valve
Reorient your thinking: The mitral valve is structurally normal.

5.

Which finding is typical on the PV loop of HOCM?
A. Increased EDV
B. Increased LV pressures with small LV volumes
C. Decreased contractility
D. Increased stroke volume

Correct answer: B
HOCM = small, stiff ventricle → high pressures, low volumes.

Click-feedback:
A. Increased EDV
Reorient your thinking: Poor filling → decreased EDV.

C. Decreased contractility
Reorient your thinking: Contractility is normal or increased.

D. Increased stroke volume
Reorient your thinking: LVOT obstruction limits SV.

6.

A patient with HOCM becomes hypotensive after starting nitroglycerin. Why?
A. Increased afterload
B. Increased LV volume
C. Reduced preload worsens LVOT obstruction
D. Increased venous return

Correct answer: C
Nitrates ↓ preload → LV cavity shrinks → obstruction worsens → ↓ cardiac output.

Click-feedback:
A. Increased afterload
Reorient your thinking: Nitrates decrease afterload.

B. Increased LV volume
Reorient your thinking: Preload reduction shrinks, not enlarges, the LV.

D. Increased venous return
Reorient your thinking: Nitrates reduce venous return via venodilation.

7.

Which medication is best for symptom control in HOCM?
A. Diuretics
B. ACE inhibitors
C. Beta-blockers
D. Digoxin

Correct answer: C — Beta-blockers
They ↓ HR, ↑ diastolic filling time, and ↓ contractility → less LVOT obstruction.

Click-feedback:
A. Diuretics
Reorient your thinking: Less preload worsens obstruction.

B. ACE inhibitors
Reorient your thinking: Afterload reduction can increase the gradient.

D. Digoxin
Reorient your thinking: Increased contractility worsens obstruction.

8.

A 19-year-old with HOCM has exertional dyspnea. Which mechanism contributes most?
A. Increased compliance
B. Impaired diastolic filling
C. Low LV pressure
D. Excess preload

Correct answer: B
Thickened myocardium fails to relax → impaired filling → ↑ filling pressures → dyspnea.

Click-feedback:
A. Increased compliance
Reorient your thinking: Hypertrophy causes reduced compliance.

C. Low LV pressure
Reorient your thinking: LV pressures are elevated, not low.

D. Excess preload
Reorient your thinking: HOCM patients typically have low preload.

9.

Which ECG finding is most associated with HOCM?
A. ST elevation
B. Deep Q waves in inferior/lateral leads
C. Peaked T waves
D. U waves

Correct answer: B
Septal hypertrophy produces pseudo–infarct Q waves.

Click-feedback:
A. ST elevation
Reorient your thinking: Suggests acute myocardial infarction.

C. Peaked T waves
Reorient your thinking: Seen in hyperkalemia.

D. U waves
Reorient your thinking: Associated with hypokalemia.

10.

Why does the HOCM murmur increase with Valsalva?
A. Increased afterload
B. Increased stroke volume
C. Decreased preload → smaller LV → more obstruction
D. Decreased contractility

Correct answer: C
Valsalva ↓ venous return → LV cavity shrinks → septum and mitral leaflet approximate → louder murmur.

Click-feedback:
A. Increased afterload
Reorient your thinking: Valsalva reduces afterload.

B. Increased stroke volume
Reorient your thinking: Stroke volume falls during Valsalva.

D. Decreased contractility
Reorient your thinking: Contractility transiently increases due to reflex sympathetic activation.

Cardiac Tamponade

1.

Cardiac tamponade most characteristically causes which hemodynamic finding?
A. Equalization of diastolic pressures
B. Increased afterload
C. Increased pulse pressure
D. Decreased venous pressure

Correct Answer: A. Equalization of diastolic pressures
B: Afterload isn’t significantly increased.
C: Pulse pressure narrows, it doesn’t widen.
D: Venous pressure increases (JVD).

2.

Which sign is most specific for cardiac tamponade?
A. S3 gallop
B. Kussmaul sign
C. Fixed splitting of S2
D. Pulsus paradoxus

Correct Answer: D. Pulsus paradoxus: During inspiration, more blood returns to the right side of the heart. But in cardiac tamponade, the heart cannot expand outward because it’s compressed by fluid. The extra right-sided filling pushes the interventricular septum toward the left ventricle, reducing LV filling. With less LV preload, stroke volume and systolic blood pressure drop during inspiration.
A: S3 = heart failure, not tamponade.
B: Kussmaul sign = constrictive pericarditis, not tamponade.
C: Fixed S2 = ASD.

3.

A patient with tamponade exhibits an absent y-descent in the JVP waveform. This is because:
A. RV filling is impaired
B. Atrial contraction is absent
C. Venous return is increased
D. RV afterload is reduced

Correct Answer: A. RV filling is impaired
B: Atrial contraction still occurs.
C: Venous return may increase on inspiration, but filling is blocked.
D: RV afterload has little relevance here.

4.

Why does systolic blood pressure fall excessively during inspiration in tamponade (pulsus paradoxus)?
A. LV preload increases during inspiration
B. RV expansion compresses the LV
C. Increased intrathoracic pressure pushes blood out of the lungs
D. LV contractility decreases

Correct Answer: B. RV expansion compresses the LV
A: LV preload decreases.
C: Intrathoracic pressure decreases, but that isn’t the primary mechanism.
D: Contractility stays normal.

5.

Which of the following best explains tachycardia in cardiac tamponade?
A. Baroreceptor response to low stroke volume
B. Increased vagal tone
C. Increased pericardial stretch receptors
D. Intrinsic SA node dysfunction

Correct Answer: A. Baroreceptor response to low stroke volume
B: Vagal tone decreases (sympathetic tone increases).
C: Pericardial receptors don’t drive tachycardia.
D: The SA node is normal.

6.

Which of the following pressure changes is most likely in tamponade?
A. Increased afterload
B. Decreased right atrial pressure
C. Narrow pulse pressure
D. Decreased sympathetic tone

Correct Answer: C. Narrow pulse pressure
A: Afterload isn’t the issue.
B: RA pressure increases.
D: Sympathetic tone rises, not falls.

7.

What is the earliest hemodynamic abnormality in developing tamponade?
A. Increased LV contractility
B. Decreased diastolic ventricular filling
C. Decreased systemic vascular resistance
D. Increased ejection fraction

Correct Answer: B. Decreased diastolic ventricular filling
A: Contractility is unchanged.
C: SVR rises due to compensation.
D: EF doesn’t increase.

8.

Which finding helps distinguish tamponade from constrictive pericarditis?
A. Elevated JVP
B. Narrow pulse pressure
C. Absent y-descent
D. Peripheral edema

Correct Answer: C. Absent y-descent
→ Constrictive pericarditis has a prominent y-descent.
A, B, D: Can be seen in either condition.

9.

In tamponade, which chamber collapses first?
A. Left atrium
B. Right atrium
C. Right ventricle
D. Left ventricle

Correct Answer: B. Right atrium
RA has the lowest pressure, so it collapses first.
A, C, D: Collapse later if tamponade worsens.

10.

Beck’s triad includes all of the following except:
A. Elevated JVP
B. Muffled heart sounds
C. Hypertension
D. Hypotension

Correct Answer: C. Hypertension
A, B, D: All are part of Beck’s triad.
C: BP is low, not high.

Cardiac Pathology: Questions & Detailed Explanations

Aortic Regurgitation

1.

Which physical exam finding is most characteristic of chronic severe aortic regurgitation?
A) S3 gallop
B) Pulsus paradoxus
C) Wide pulse pressure with bounding pulses
D) Jugular venous distension

Correct answer: C
Chronic AR causes increased stroke volume (due to LV dilation and high preload) and rapid diastolic runoff back into the LV → high systolic pressure + low diastolic pressure → wide pulse pressure and bounding pulses.

Click-feedback (physiology-focused):
A) S3 gallop
Reorient your thinking:
An S3 reflects rapid early diastolic filling into a volume-overloaded ventricle, which can occur in AR but is not specific. AR’s hallmark findings are driven by pulse pressure abnormalities, not filling sounds.

B) Pulsus paradoxus
Reorient your thinking:
Pulsus paradoxus occurs when inspiration reduces LV filling due to external constraint (e.g., tamponade). In AR, the problem is diastolic backflow through the aortic valve, not ventricular interdependence or pericardial constraint.

D) Jugular venous distension
Reorient your thinking:
JVD reflects elevated right atrial pressure. Chronic AR primarily causes left-sided volume overload; right-sided pressures rise only very late after severe LV failure.

2.

Which is the most common cause of chronic aortic regurgitation in developed countries?
A) Rheumatic heart disease
B) Bicuspid aortic valve
C) Infective endocarditis
D) Mitral valve prolapse

Correct answer: B
Bicuspid valves undergo abnormal mechanical stress → early degeneration → valve incompetence, making them the most common cause in developed nations.

Click-feedback:
A) Rheumatic heart disease
Reorient your thinking:
Rheumatic disease causes leaflet fibrosis and commissural fusion, typically producing stenosis or mixed lesions. Its prevalence is lower in developed countries.

C) Infective endocarditis
Reorient your thinking:
Endocarditis causes AR through acute leaflet destruction, but it is an episodic cause, not the most common etiology of chronic AR.

D) Mitral valve prolapse
Reorient your thinking:
MVP affects the mitral valve, leading to MR, not incompetence of the aortic valve or diastolic backflow into the LV.

3.

Which echocardiographic parameter best defines severe aortic regurgitation?
A) Jet width <25% of LVOT
B) Vena contracta >0.6 cm
C) Regurgitant volume <30 mL/beat
D) ERO <0.10 cm²

Correct answer: B
The vena contracta represents the narrowest regurgitant orifice, making it a relatively load-independent marker of severity.

Click-feedback:
A) Jet width <25% of LVOT
Reorient your thinking:
A narrow jet implies limited regurgitant flow, reflecting low regurgitant volume, consistent with mild AR.

C) Regurgitant volume <30 mL/beat
Reorient your thinking:
Regurgitant volume quantifies backflow per beat. Severe AR requires large regurgitant volumes, not small ones.

D) ERO <0.10 cm²
Reorient your thinking:
Effective regurgitant orifice area reflects the size of the valve defect. A small ERO cannot generate severe volume overload.

4.

What is the primary hemodynamic consequence of chronic AR?
A) LV pressure overload only
B) LV volume overload only
C) Combined LV volume and pressure overload
D) RV volume overload

Correct answer: C
Chronic AR causes volume overload from regurgitant blood and pressure overload from high stroke volumes needed to maintain forward flow.

Click-feedback:
A) LV pressure overload only
Reorient your thinking:
Pressure overload alone (e.g., AS) does not account for the diastolic regurgitant volume returning to the LV.

B) LV volume overload only
Reorient your thinking:
The LV must eject a larger total volume each beat, increasing systolic wall stress and pressure demand.

D) RV volume overload
Reorient your thinking:
The regurgitant lesion directly affects the left ventricle; RV overload occurs only secondarily in end-stage disease.

5.

Which symptom is most likely to develop late in chronic AR?
A) Dyspnea
B) Syncope
C) Palpitations
D) Hemoptysis

Correct answer: A
Dyspnea develops when LV compensatory dilation fails, causing elevated LVEDP → ↑ LA pressure → pulmonary congestion.

Click-feedback:
B) Syncope
Reorient your thinking:
Syncope is classically related to fixed outflow obstruction (AS), not regurgitant lesions.

C) Palpitations
Reorient your thinking:
Palpitations may occur earlier due to hyperdynamic circulation, but do not signal late decompensation.

D) Hemoptysis
Reorient your thinking:
Hemoptysis suggests pulmonary hemorrhage or embolism, not LV volume overload.

6.

Which is the most important diagnostic test for AR?
A) Chest X-ray
B) Echocardiography
C) Cardiac MRI
D) CT angiography

Correct answer: B
Echo directly visualizes valve morphology, regurgitant flow, and LV response, which defines severity and timing of surgery.

Click-feedback:
A) Chest X-ray
Reorient your thinking:
CXR may show cardiomegaly but cannot assess regurgitant severity or valve dynamics.

C) Cardiac MRI
Reorient your thinking:
MRI quantifies volume accurately but is second-line after echocardiography.

D) CT angiography
Reorient your thinking:
CT assesses anatomy, not hemodynamic severity of regurgitation.

7.

Which finding is most specific for severe AR on physical exam?
A) Austin Flint murmur
B) Systolic ejection murmur
C) Loud P2
D) Pericardial rub

Correct answer: A
Austin Flint murmur occurs when regurgitant flow impinges on the mitral valve, functionally mimicking mitral stenosis — a marker of severe AR.

Click-feedback:
B) Systolic ejection murmur
Reorient your thinking:
This reflects increased forward flow, not severity of regurgitation.

C) Loud P2
Reorient your thinking:
Loud P2 reflects pulmonary hypertension, not primary aortic valve disease.

D) Pericardial rub
Reorient your thinking:
A friction rub arises from inflamed pericardial surfaces, unrelated to valvular incompetence.

8.

In chronic severe AR, when is surgery indicated according to guidelines?
A) Only when symptoms develop
B) When LVEF <55% or LVESD >50 mm
C) When mild LV dilation is present
D) Only if regurgitant volume >30 mL/beat

Correct answer: B
Surgery is timed to prevent irreversible LV remodeling, not to wait for overt failure.

Click-feedback:
A) Only when symptoms develop
Reorient your thinking:
Symptoms appear after LV dysfunction has already begun, making this too late.

C) When mild LV dilation is present
Reorient your thinking:
Mild dilation reflects compensation; surgery is based on thresholds signaling decompensation.

D) Only if regurgitant volume >30 mL/beat
Reorient your thinking:
Regurgitant volume measures severity but does not reflect LV tolerance or function.

9.

Which peripheral sign is NOT associated with severe AR?
A) Quincke’s pulse
B) Musset’s sign
C) Hill’s sign
D) Kussmaul’s sign

Correct answer: D
Kussmaul’s sign reflects impaired RV filling, not systemic arterial runoff.

Click-feedback:
A) Quincke’s pulse
Reorient your thinking:
Capillary pulsations result from wide pulse pressure.

B) Musset’s sign
Reorient your thinking:
Head bobbing reflects forceful systolic ejection.

C) Hill’s sign
Reorient your thinking:
Elevated leg systolic BP reflects amplified pulse pressure.

10.

What is the role of vasodilator therapy in chronic AR?
A) First-line for all patients
B) To delay surgery in asymptomatic patients with normal LV function
C) To treat acute AR
D) To prevent infective endocarditis

Correct answer: B
Vasodilators reduce afterload, lowering regurgitant fraction and LV wall stress.

Click-feedback:
A) First-line for all patients
Reorient your thinking:
Surgery is definitive; vasodilators are adjunctive.

C) To treat acute AR
Reorient your thinking:
Acute AR causes sudden volume overload with no time for compensation → emergent surgery.

D) To prevent infective endocarditis
Reorient your thinking:
Vasodilators do not affect infection risk or valve colonization.

Aortic Stenosis

11.

Which symptom is most closely associated with severe aortic stenosis?
Correct Answer: A) Angina
Severe AS causes marked LV pressure overload → concentric hypertrophy → increased myocardial oxygen demand, while simultaneously reducing coronary perfusion pressure due to elevated LVEDP.

Analysis of Incorrect Choices (Physiology-Focused)
B) Palpitations
Reorient your thinking:
Palpitations reflect arrhythmias or heightened sympathetic tone, neither of which is intrinsic to the fixed outflow obstruction of AS. AS symptoms arise from inability to augment cardiac output, not rhythm abnormalities.

C) Cough
Reorient your thinking:
Cough is typically due to airway irritation or pulmonary congestion. In AS, pulmonary congestion occurs only late, after LV failure, making cough nonspecific and indirect.

D) Hemoptysis
Reorient your thinking:

Shock Questions

Question 1

A trauma patient presents with hypotension, tachycardia, and cool, clammy skin. Which hemodynamic change is most characteristic of hypovolemic shock?
A) CO ↓, SVR ↑, PCWP ↓
B) CO ↑, SVR ↓, PCWP ↑
C) CO ↑, SVR ↑, PCWP ↑
D) CO ↓, SVR ↓, PCWP ↑

Correct Answer: A) CO ↓, SVR ↑, PCWP ↓
Explanation: Hypovolemic shock is caused by a significant loss of intravascular volume (e.g., hemorrhage or severe dehydration). The reduced circulating volume leads to decreased preload and thus a drop in cardiac output (CO), since the heart has less blood to pump. With less blood filling the left side of the heart, the pulmonary capillary wedge pressure (PCWP) (a measure of left atrial pressure) is low. In response, the body activates the sympathetic nervous system via baroreceptors, causing vasoconstriction and an increase in systemic vascular resistance (SVR) to maintain perfusion. This compensatory vasoconstriction (along with tachycardia) is why patients in hypovolemic shock have cool, clammy skin.
Incorrect Answer Choices:
B) This hemodynamic profile indicates increased cardiac output with decreased SVR, which is characteristic of early distributive shock (e.g., septic shock), not hypovolemic shock. Moreover, a high PCWP suggests elevated left heart filling pressure as seen in cardiogenic shock, which contradicts the low filling pressures in hypovolemia.
C) No shock state presents with simultaneously elevated CO, SVR, and PCWP. If PCWP were high (indicating elevated left-sided filling pressure), a failing pump (as in cardiogenic shock) would typically cause low CO rather than a high CO. Thus, this combination is not physiologically consistent with hypovolemic shock.
D) This option combines features that don’t occur together in hypovolemic shock. Decreased SVR is seen in distributive shock (septic or neurogenic), not in hypovolemia, and an elevated PCWP indicates pump failure (cardiogenic shock). In true hypovolemic shock, SVR should be increased (compensatory vasoconstriction) and PCWP would be low due to lack of volume.

Question 2

Which of the following findings is most specific for obstructive shock due to cardiac tamponade?
A) CVP ↑, CO ↓, SVR ↑
B) CVP ↓, CO ↑, SVR ↓
C) CVP ↑, CO ↑, SVR ↓
D) CVP ↓, CO ↓, SVR ↑

Correct Answer: A) CVP ↑, CO ↓, SVR ↑
Explanation: Cardiac tamponade (an obstructive form of shock) involves fluid buildup in the pericardium that restricts ventricular filling. As a result, blood backs up into the venous system, causing an increase in central venous pressure (CVP) (clinically evident as jugular venous distension). Inadequate filling of the heart leads to a decreased cardiac output (CO). To compensate for the falling blood pressure, the sympathetic nervous system raises the systemic vascular resistance (SVR) through vasoconstriction. Thus, the combination of elevated CVP, low CO, and high SVR is characteristic of cardiac tamponade–induced obstructive shock.
Incorrect Answer Choices:
B) This choice shows low CVP with high CO and low SVR, which is the opposite of tamponade’s hemodynamics. A low CVP and low SVR are seen in distributive shock (e.g., early sepsis, where vasodilation causes both decreased venous return and decreased SVR) along with a reflex high CO. This profile does not match cardiac tamponade.
C) This combination is not physiologically coherent for tamponade. Elevated CVP with decreased SVR would imply mixed features of different shock types (e.g., volume overload or right heart failure plus vasodilation). Tamponade should cause high CVP with high SVR (due to compensation) and a low CO – it would not present with a high CO and low SVR.
D) This profile (low CVP, low CO, high SVR) actually matches hypovolemic shock, not tamponade. In hypovolemia, low intravascular volume leads to low CVP and low CO with reflex SVR increase. In cardiac tamponade, CVP would be elevated (from impaired filling and venous backup), so option D is describing the wrong shock type.

Question 3

What happens to mixed venous oxygen saturation (SvO₂) in hypovolemic shock?
A) SvO₂ ↓
B) SvO₂ ↑
C) SvO₂ unchanged
D) SvO₂ variable

Correct Answer: A) SvO₂ ↓
Explanation: In hypovolemic shock, cardiac output is low, so less oxygenated blood is delivered to tissues. As a result, tissues extract more oxygen from the blood to meet their needs, leaving the venous blood with a lower oxygen saturation. Therefore, the mixed venous O₂ saturation (SvO₂) decreases in hypovolemia (less O₂ remains in the venous blood).
Incorrect Answer Choices:
B) An elevated SvO₂ indicates that tissues are extracting less oxygen than normal, leaving more O₂ in the venous blood. This occurs in conditions like early septic shock (high-flow states with impaired oxygen extraction), not in hypovolemic shock.
C) If the body is in shock from low flow, SvO₂ would not stay normal. In hypovolemic shock, the significant reduction in CO causes increased extraction and a drop in SvO₂, so it would not remain unchanged.
D) SvO₂ does not fluctuate randomly in hypovolemic shock – it predictably decreases due to increased oxygen extraction. Describing it as “variable” is incorrect; the expected change is a decrease in SvO₂.

Question 4

Which of the following best distinguishes hypovolemic from cardiogenic shock?
A) PCWP ↓ in hypovolemic, ↑ in cardiogenic
B) PCWP ↑ in hypovolemic, ↓ in cardiogenic
C) SVR ↓ in hypovolemic, ↑ in cardiogenic
D) CO ↑ in hypovolemic, ↓ in cardiogenic

Correct Answer: A) PCWP ↓ in hypovolemic, ↑ in cardiogenic
Explanation: A key difference between these shock types is in the filling pressures of the left heart: Hypovolemic shockinvolves low intravascular volume, so PCWP is low (the heart’s filling pressure is reduced). In cardiogenic shock, the failing left ventricle causes blood to back up into the pulmonary circulation, so PCWP is elevated. Thus, low PCWP (in hypovolemia) vs high PCWP (in cardiogenic shock) best distinguishes the two.
Incorrect Answer Choices:
B) This choice is the exact opposite of the true scenario. Hypovolemic shock would not have an elevated PCWP – it would have a low PCWP due to lack of volume. Conversely, cardiogenic shock does not have a low PCWP – it typically has an elevated PCWP from blood backing up.
C) This is incorrect because systemic vascular resistance (SVR) is not decreased in hypovolemic shock – in fact, it is increased (due to vasoconstrictive compensation). Both hypovolemic and cardiogenic shock typically feature increased SVR as a compensatory response, so SVR is not what differentiates them in this way.
D) This is false because cardiac output (CO) is actually decreased in hypovolemic shock (low preload leads to low CO), not increased. Cardiogenic shock does indeed have a low CO, but hypovolemia would never present with an increased CO. Thus, option D misstates the hemodynamics of hypovolemia.

Question 5

A patient with an acute anterior MI develops hypotension, pulmonary edema, and cold extremities. Which hemodynamic profile is most consistent with cardiogenic shock?
A) CO ↓, SVR ↑, PCWP ↑
B) CO ↑, SVR ↓, PCWP ↓
C) CO ↑, SVR ↑, PCWP ↓
D) CO ↓, SVR ↓, PCWP ↓

Correct Answer: A) CO ↓, SVR ↑, PCWP ↑
Explanation: In this acute myocardial infarction scenario, the damaged left ventricle cannot pump effectively, so cardiac output (CO) falls. The blood backs up into the lungs, causing elevated pulmonary capillary wedge pressure (PCWP)(reflecting pulmonary edema). To compensate for the low blood pressure, SVR increases (systemic vasoconstriction via sympathetic activation). Thus, cardiogenic shock is marked by low CO, high PCWP, and high SVR.
Incorrect Answer Choices:
B) This choice (high CO, low SVR, low PCWP) describes a distributive shock picture (like early septic shock, where vasodilation causes low SVR and often a high CO). Cardiogenic shock would not present with an increased cardiac output or reduced SVR, so option B is incorrect.
C) Cardiogenic shock would not have an increased CO – the hallmark is decreased CO due to pump failure. A combination of high CO, high SVR, and low PCWP does not correspond to any typical shock state (and certainly not cardiogenic shock), making this option wrong.
D) This combination (low CO, low SVR, low PCWP) is not consistent with cardiogenic shock. Low SVR indicates widespread vasodilation, which is not a feature of early cardiogenic shock (SVR would be high from sympathetic compensation). Question 6

A patient with hypovolemic shock is given IV fluids. Which of the following changes is expected?
A) Preload ↑, CO ↑
B) Preload ↓, CO ↓
C) Preload ↑, CO ↓
D) Preload ↓, CO ↑

Correct Answer: A) Preload ↑, CO ↑
Explanation: IV fluids restore intravascular volume, increasing preload (filling of the heart). With more volume returning to the heart, the Frank-Starling mechanism allows the heart to pump more forcefully, so cardiac output (CO) increases. Thus, fluids raise both the preload and, as a result, the CO in hypovolemia.
Incorrect Answer Choices:
B) This choice is the opposite of what fluids do. Decreased preload and CO would occur if volume were taken away, not added. IV fluids should not decrease preload or CO; they increase them.
C) This suggests fluids increased preload but somehow decreased CO, which is not expected in hypovolemia. In a volume-depleted patient with a healthy heart, more preload leads to higher CO. A decrease in CO despite higher preload might occur in severe heart failure (fluid overload in cardiogenic shock), but not in hypovolemic shock with an intact heart.
D) It is not physiologically plausible for preload to decrease while CO increases. If preload went down (less venous return), CO would also drop. Therefore, this option does not make sense for any volume infusion scenario and is incorrect.

Question 7

In distributive shock, what is the typical effect on mixed venous oxygen saturation (SvO₂)?
A) SvO₂ ↑
B) SvO₂ ↓
C) SvO₂ unchanged
D) SvO₂ variable

Correct Answer: A) SvO₂ ↑
Explanation: In distributive shock such as sepsis, systemic vasodilation and often increased cardiac output result in tissues extracting less oxygen from blood than normal. Additionally, cellular dysfunction in septic shock can impair oxygen uptake. Consequently, the blood returning to the heart has a higher SvO₂ (venous oxygen saturation is elevated because less O₂ is removed by tissues).
Incorrect Answer Choices:
B) A decrease in SvO₂ is seen in low-output states (like cardiogenic or hypovolemic shock) where tissues extract extra oxygen due to reduced flow. In early distributive shock (septic shock), CO is often high and peripheral tissues can’t extract all the oxygen, so SvO₂ is high, not low.
C) It is unlikely for SvO₂ to remain normal in significant septic shock. With profound changes in perfusion and extraction, SvO₂ typically rises (in early sepsis). Therefore, an “unchanged” SvO₂ is not characteristic of distributive shock.
D) Describing SvO₂ as “variable” does not identify the key physiologic change. In distributive (septic) shock, SvO₂ tends to be elevated (not unpredictably variable) due to increased blood flow and impaired extraction. Thus, this option is not the best description.

Question 8

In massive PE, which hemodynamic change is most likely?
A) CO ↓, SVR ↑, CVP ↑
B) CO ↑, SVR ↓, CVP ↓
C) CO ↑, SVR ↑, CVP ↓
D) CO ↓, SVR ↓, CVP ↓

Correct Answer: A) CO ↓, SVR ↑, CVP ↑
Explanation: A massive pulmonary embolism causes an acute blockage in the pulmonary circulation, which reduces blood flow from the right heart to the left heart. The result is a sharp decrease in cardiac output (CO). Blood backs up behind the obstruction, leading to elevated central venous pressure (CVP) (high pressure in the venous system, often manifesting as jugular venous distension). The body responds to the drop in arterial pressure by raising SVR (systemic vascular resistance) via sympathetic vasoconstriction. So, a massive PE produces low CO, high CVP, and a compensatory increase in SVR.
Incorrect Answer Choices:
B) This profile (high CO, low SVR, low CVP) is characteristic of distributive shock (e.g., septic shock), not an obstructive shock like pulmonary embolism. A massive PE would not cause an increased CO or systemic vasodilation – it causes the opposite (low CO with reflex vasoconstriction).
C) This combination is not typical of any shock state from a PE. A high CO would not occur in a massive PE (CO would fall), and a low CVP is incorrect for PE because venous pressure would rise due to right ventricular outflow obstruction. Thus, option C is inconsistent with PE physiology.
D) This shows all values decreased, which is not what happens in a massive PE. While CO would decrease, a massive PE triggers a rise in SVR (not a decrease) via sympathetic output, and CVP would be elevated (not low) because blood is backing up in the venous system. All parameters low is more suggestive of neurogenic shock (loss of sympathetic tone), not PE.

Question 9

Which compensatory response is most prominent in early hypovolemic shock?
A) Sympathetic activation → SVR ↑
B) Parasympathetic activation → HR ↓
C) RAAS inhibition → Na⁺ excretion ↑
D) Vasodilation → SVR ↓

Correct Answer: A) Sympathetic activation → SVR ↑
Explanation: In the initial stage of hypovolemia, the drop in blood pressure triggers the baroreceptor reflex, leading to increased sympathetic outflow. This sympathetic activation causes vasoconstriction (raising SVR) and tachycardia to maintain perfusion to vital organs. Thus, early hypovolemic shock is characterized by a surge in sympathetic activity causing an increase in SVR (and heart rate).
Incorrect Answer Choices:
B) This is the opposite of what occurs. Parasympathetic (vagal) activation leading to a slowed heart rate is not a compensatory response in shock – in fact, it would worsen hypotension. Early shock prompts sympathetic activation (increased heart rate), not parasympathetic slowing of the heart.
C) In hypovolemia, the body activates the renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system (RAAS) to conserve water and sodium; it would not inhibit RAAS. In other words, early shock causes increased renin release (leading to sodium retention and vasoconstriction), not RAAS inhibition with increased Na⁺ excretion. So this option is incorrect.
D) Vasodilation (decreased SVR) is not a compensatory mechanism in hypovolemic shock; it would actually make the hypotension worse. Early hypovolemic shock is marked by vasoconstriction (increased SVR) due to sympathetic discharge, not vasodilation.

Question 10

A patient with septic shock is most likely to have which of the following initial hemodynamic changes?
A) CO ↑, SVR ↓, PCWP normal/↓
B) CO ↓, SVR ↑, PCWP ↑
C) CO ↓, SVR ↓, PCWP ↑
D) CO ↑, SVR ↑, PCWP ↑

Correct Answer: A) CO ↑, SVR ↓, PCWP normal/↓
Explanation: In the early phase of septic shock (a distributive shock), widespread vasodilation from inflammatory mediators causes a sharp decrease in SVR. The reduced afterload, along with direct effects of cytokines, often leads to a compensatory increase in cardiac output (CO) in this hyperdynamic phase. PCWP is usually normal or low because the issue is not cardiac pump failure or fluid overload – in fact, venous pooling can reduce preload. Thus, early septic shock typically presents with low SVR, elevated or normal CO, and normal to low PCWP.
Incorrect Answer Choices:
B) This hemodynamic profile (low CO, high SVR, high PCWP) is actually that of cardiogenic shock (pump failure). In septic shock, CO is not low in the initial phase and SVR is certainly not high – septic shock has low SVR. So option B does not describe early septic shock.
C) This option mixes findings that don’t occur together in septic shock. The elevated PCWP suggests cardiogenic pulmonary edema, but the low SVR suggests distributive shock. Septic shock would not cause a high PCWP (the heart is not failing initially), so this combination is incorrect for early sepsis.
D) All values elevated is not the pattern for septic shock. In early sepsis, SVR is low (due to vasodilation), so it wouldn’t be increased. A simultaneously high CO, high SVR, and high PCWP does not correspond to septic shock (and in fact, no typical shock state presents with everything increased). Therefore, D is incorrect.

Question 11

Question 12

What happens to PCWP in cardiogenic shock?
A) PCWP ↑
B) PCWP ↓
C) PCWP unchanged
D) PCWP variable

Correct Answer: A) PCWP ↑
Explanation: Pulmonary capillary wedge pressure (PCWP) reflects left atrial pressure. In cardiogenic shock (especially from left ventricular failure), the left ventricle cannot pump blood forward effectively, causing blood to accumulate in the left atrium and pulmonary veins. This leads to an elevated PCWP (high left-sided filling pressure). In short, cardiogenic shock is associated with a high PCWP due to pulmonary congestion from the failing left heart.

Question 13

Which physical finding is most specific for hypovolemic shock?
A) Supine hypotension
B) Moist axilla
C) Prolonged capillary refill
D) S3 gallop

Correct Answer: A) Supine hypotension
Explanation: In a person with severe hypovolemia, even lying flat (supine) does not bring up enough venous return to normalize blood pressure. Supine hypotension (low blood pressure when the patient is lying down) strongly suggests a markedly reduced circulating volume – under normal circumstances, the supine position increases venous return and helps maintain blood pressure. If a patient is still hypotensive while supine, it indicates that intravascular volume is critically low, which is specific for hypovolemic shock.
Incorrect Answer Choices:
B) A “moist axilla” simply indicates that the patient is sweaty. Sweating (diaphoresis) can occur in many shock states due to sympathetic activation – it’s not unique to hypovolemia. For example, patients in cardiogenic, obstructive, or even distributive shock can also be cold and sweaty. Therefore, this finding is not specific to hypovolemic shock.
C) Prolonged capillary refill (slow return of color after blanching a fingertip) signals poor peripheral perfusion. While this is common in hypovolemic shock, it also occurs in other shock types (e.g., cardiogenic or obstructive shock) where output is low. It is a general sign of decreased perfusion and is not uniquely indicative of hypovolemia.
D) An S3 gallop is a sign of increased left ventricular end-diastolic volume and heart failure (the sound of blood sloshing into an overfilled ventricle). This finding is typically heard in cardiogenic shock (acute heart failure), not in hypovolemic shock (where the heart chambers are under-filled). Thus, an S3 points to pump failure rather than volume loss.

Question 14

B) S3 gallop

Question 16

Which hemodynamic parameter is most associated with mortality in septic shock?
A) SVR ↓
B) CO ↑
C) PCWP ↑
D) HR ↓

Correct Answer: A) SVR ↓

Question 25

What hemodynamic change is most characteristic of hypovolemic shock?

B) CO ↑, SVR ↓, PCWP ↑
C) CO ↑, SVR ↑, PCWP ↑

Correct Answer: A) CO ↓, SVR ↑, PCWP ↓

Explanation:
Hypovolemic shock is due to significant volume loss (bleeding, dehydration, etc.), leading to low preload. With less venous return, the heart’s output (CO) drops. Question 27
